ACTIVE TRANSPORT

Learning Active Transport, the Smart Way

What is Active Transport?

Active Transport in biology refers to the movement of molecules or ions across a cell membrane from a region of lower concentration to a region of higher concentration or against the concentration gradient. This process requires energy, typically in the form of ATP (adenosine triphosphate).
Active Transport moves substances across the cell membrane against their concentration gradient (from low to high concentration), which requires energy.

How it Works

Types of Active Transport

Sodium-Potassium Pump

The Sodium-Potassium Pump is a protein in cell membranes that uses ATP to move sodium ions out and potassium ions into the cell, maintaining essential gradients for nerve impulses and muscle contraction.

Bulk Transport

Bulk Transport moves large particles or fluids via vesicles. Endocytosis brings substances into the cell, while exocytosis expels them. Both require energy and are vital for cell function.
